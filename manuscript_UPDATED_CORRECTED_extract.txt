Immediate Effects of a Single Session of PETTLEP-Based Action Observation and Motor Imagery (AOMI) on Upper Limb Kinematics in Stroke Survivors: A Randomized Controlled Trial

ABSTRACT
Background:Stroke ranks as the second leading cause of death and a principal cause of adult disability worldwide, with persistent upper limb motor impairment reported in up to two-thirds of survivors. Pathological movement patterns — characterized by jerky multi-peaked velocity trajectories, trunk compensation, and shoulder girdle elevation — reflect disruptions in cortical motor planning and execution that extend beyond simple weakness to encompass disordered feedforward postural control, abnormal movement synergies, and spinal reflex dysregulation. Conventional task-based training is often inaccessible to individuals with severe motor deficits, highlighting the need for cognitively mediated alternatives. Action Observation combined with Motor Imagery (AOMI), structured within the PETTLEP framework, represents a promising approach for activating motor pathways without requiring overt physical effort. However, the immediate kinematic effects of a single AOMI session — observation followed by imagery without interleaved physical practice — have not been rigorously investigated using objective, single-camera markerless kinematic analysis.
Objective:This study aimed to investigate whether a single session of PETTLEP-based AOMI produces immediate improvements in movement smoothness (smoothness_pause_pct; primary outcome), total movement duration (total_duration_s), trunk-to-palm displacement ratio (total_trunk_palm_ratio), shoulder vertical displacement range (shoulder_vert_norm), peak hand velocity (total_peak_velocity), and upper limb functional performance (WMFT-4) compared to a time-matched cognitive and somatic control condition in individuals post-stroke.
Methods:A single-blind, pretest–posttest, prospective randomized controlled trial (RCT) was designed for conduct at the Neurorehabilitation Clinic of Istinye University Liv Bahçeşehir Hospital, Istanbul, Türkiye. A total of 28 stroke survivors (14 per group) aged 40–80 years, with mild-to-moderate spasticity (Modified Ashworth Scale ≤ 2) and residual voluntary upper limb movement, were to be recruited and randomized to either the PETTLEP-based AOMI group or a cognitive and somatic control group. The AOMI protocol consisted of a 2-minute sensorimotor transfer calibration phase followed by five 4-minute blocks (20 minutes total), each comprising 1 minute of action observation, 2 minutes of guided corrective motor imagery, and 1 minute of rest. The five blocks progressively targeted: (1) movement initiation, (2) trunk stability, (3) shoulder girdle control, (4) elbow opening and muscle quieting, and (5) integrated smooth movement. Participants used a "notice–name–transfer" strategy to map kinesthetic sensations from the unaffected to the affected limb. The control group performed time-matched body scanning and spatial navigation tasks (22 minutes total). Two-dimensional pose-landmark kinematics of the affected upper limb were to be captured using a custom markerless motion capture pipeline based on MediaPipe Pose Landmarker (Google) for 2D landmark extraction from a single smartphone or webcam at 30 fps and 1080p resolution, combined with the ZoeDepth monocular depth estimation network for metric scaling. The primary outcome was movement smoothness (smoothness_pause_pct) — the percentage of time within the active movement window where hand velocity falls below a fixed normalized threshold (0.03), indexing movement intermittency and stop-and-go behavior. Secondary outcomes included total movement duration (total_duration_s), peak and mean hand velocity, trunk-to-palm displacement ratio, maximum elbow angle, total path length, lateral hand range, shoulder vertical displacement range (shoulder_vert_norm; cm equivalent via ZoeDepth metric scaling, exported as total_depression_cm), phase-specific metrics (forward, wipe_right, wipe_left, return), Wolf Motor Function Test – 4-item short form (WMFT-4), Visual Analog Mood Scale – 4 dimensions (VAMS-4), Kinesthetic and Visual Imagery Questionnaire – 10 items (KVIQ-10), International Physical Activity Questionnaire (IPAQ), Motor Difference Rating Scale (MDRS, post-intervention only), and Visual Analog Scale for pain (VAS). All kinematic variables were collected and managed using the NeuroLab Stroke Rehabilitation Research Platform (custom web application; kinematics_analyzer.py v6). Data were to be analyzed using a 2 × 2 Mixed-Model ANOVA, with non-parametric alternatives applied where indicated.
Expected Results:It was hypothesized that the PETTLEP-based AOMI group would demonstrate significantly greater reductions in movement intermittency (lower smoothness_pause_pct), trunk compensation (lower total_trunk_palm_ratio), and shoulder girdle elevation, as well as shorter total movement duration and improved upper limb functional performance (WMFT-4) compared to the control group.
Conclusion:This study was designed to provide novel, objectively measured kinematic evidence for the immediate neuromotor effects of a single PETTLEP-based AOMI session in stroke survivors, informing the clinical integration of cognitively mediated motor rehabilitation strategies.
Keywords:Action observation; motor imagery; AOMI; PETTLEP; stroke; upper limb rehabilitation; kinematics; MediaPipe; movement smoothness; markerless motion capture.
1. INTRODUCTION
1.1 Stroke and Upper Limb Disability: The Scope of the Problem
Stroke remains one of the most devastating neurological conditions globally, ranking as the second leading cause of death and a leading cause of adult disability worldwide (1). According to the World Stroke Organization's 2022 Global Stroke Fact Sheet, stroke affects approximately 12.2 million individuals annually, with over 101 million people currently living with stroke-related impairments (1). The epidemiological burden is particularly pronounced in low- and middle-income countries, where healthcare resources for long-term rehabilitation are often insufficient. In Türkiye, national data from 2019 indicate a stroke incidence of approximately 125,345 cases per year, with a prevalence exceeding 1.08 million individuals — the majority being women and those over 50 years of age (2).
Among the spectrum of post-stroke deficits, upper limb motor impairment is particularly prevalent and functionally disabling. Approximately 70–80% of stroke survivors present with hemiparesis affecting the upper extremity in the acute phase, and more than two-thirds of these individuals experience persistent long-term deficits in upper limb motor function that restrict their ability to perform activities of daily living (ADL), reduce productivity, and limit social participation (3, 4). These consequences extend beyond the individual, imposing a global economic burden exceeding $891 billion annually (1).
The pathophysiology of upper limb motor impairment post-stroke is multifactorial. Disruption of cortical and subcortical motor pathways — particularly the corticospinal tract — leads to weakness or paralysis, altered muscle tone (spasticity), and the emergence of pathological movement synergies (5). Over time, these neurological deficits interact with secondary musculoskeletal adaptations, including shortened muscle fascicles, increased intramuscular fibrosis, and elevated passive muscle stiffness — all of which further restrict voluntary movement and contribute to chronic functional impairment (6, 7).
A particularly clinically relevant manifestation of these deficits is the quality of voluntary upper limb movement. Whereas healthy individuals produce smooth, bell-shaped velocity profiles during reaching tasks — reflecting efficient feedforward motor planning and execution — stroke survivors characteristically produce fragmented, multi-peaked velocity trajectories indicative of impaired motor control (8). These velocity peaks, or submovements, reflect the nervous system's iterative attempts to correct ongoing movement errors through feedback-based adjustments, a process that becomes dominant when feedforward planning is disrupted. Movement smoothness indices derived from hand velocity profiles — including pause percentage (smoothness_pause_pct) — have been proposed as sensitive, objective biomarkers of upper limb motor recovery (8). In parallel, compensatory strategies such as trunk forward lean and shoulder girdle elevation during reaching tasks are widely observed and reflect the brain's reorganized, albeit maladaptive, attempt to overcome restricted arm reach (9, 10).
Restoring upper limb motor function and movement quality — rather than merely enabling coarse task completion — is therefore a central objective of contemporary stroke rehabilitation.
1.2 Motor Control Foundations: From Feedforward Postural Control to Movement Initiation
To understand why the upper limb movement quality deteriorates after stroke, and why cognitively mediated interventions such as AOMI may be capable of addressing this deterioration, it is necessary to consider the hierarchical organization of normal reaching from a motor control perspective.
In healthy individuals, voluntary reaching is not initiated simply by activating the prime movers of the arm. Rather, normal reaching follows a proximal-to-distal organizational principle in which the central nervous system prepares postural stability before permitting limb movement. This preparation manifests as anticipatory postural adjustments (APAs) — feedforward activations of trunk and proximal musculature that occur 50–100 milliseconds prior to the onset of voluntary arm movement (20, 21). APAs have been documented consistently in studies of voluntary reaching, where activation of trunk extensor and abdominal muscles precedes deltoid activation, thereby stabilizing the base of support before the destabilizing forces of arm movement are introduced (20).
The neural substrates of this feedforward organization are well established. The supplementary motor area (SMA) plays a central role in the temporal sequencing of trunk stabilization and limb movement initiation, while corticoreticular projections from the motor cortex modulate the activity of the reticular formation in the brainstem, which in turn governs reticulospinal output to axial and proximal musculature (22, 23). The corticospinal tract, by contrast, provides the selective, fractionated control of distal musculature — including the fine-grained recruitment of forearm and hand muscles — that characterizes skilled reaching and manipulation (22).
This hierarchical architecture — feedforward trunk stabilization preceding distal movement execution — has direct relevance to the present study. After stroke, disruption of corticospinal and corticoreticular pathways produces a characteristic breakdown of this hierarchy. Trunk APAs are reduced or delayed, shoulder girdle stabilization is impaired, and the selective corticospinal drive to distal muscles is weakened (24). The consequence is a movement that relies on compensatory trunk lean and shoulder elevation to substitute for deficient proximal stability and distal reach — precisely the kinematic signatures that this study was designed to measure and modify.
Understanding this proximal-to-distal principle further informed the sequential structure of the PETTLEP-based AOMI protocol employed in the present study. The five progressive imagery blocks were ordered to mirror the physiological hierarchy of normal reaching: movement initiation first, followed by trunk stability, then shoulder girdle control, then elbow opening, and finally integrated smooth movement. This ordering reflects the neurophysiological logic of APA-first, limb-action-second, and was chosen to maximize the ecological validity and motor specificity of the imagery protocol.
1.3 Neurophysiology of Post-Stroke Upper Limb Impairment
The motor deficits observed after stroke result from disruption at multiple levels of the motor control hierarchy. Loss of corticospinal integrity produces not only weakness but also a failure of selective motor control — the ability to activate individual muscles independently of synergistic patterns. After stroke, particularly when lesions involve the internal capsule or corona radiata, the relative influence of phylogenetically older, less fractionated descending pathways — including the reticulospinal and vestibulospinal tracts — increases relative to the corticospinal system (22, 25). This shift in descending pathway balance underlies the emergence of obligatory movement synergies, in which voluntary activation of one muscle group obligatorily recruits others in stereotyped patterns: shoulder adduction and internal rotation coupled with elbow flexion, forearm pronation, and wrist and finger flexion in the upper limb (25).
Alongside synergy-related impairments, post-stroke spasticity represents a major contributor to movement dysfunction. Spasticity, defined classically as a velocity-dependent increase in tonic stretch reflex activity resulting from hyperexcitability of the stretch reflex as a component of the upper motor neuron syndrome (26), arises from the loss of descending inhibitory modulation of spinal interneuronal circuits. Specifically, the reduction of corticospinal and corticoreticular input to the spinal cord results in decreased presynaptic inhibition of Ia afferent terminals, reduced reciprocal Ia inhibition between antagonist muscle pairs, and increased excitability of alpha motor neurons — collectively producing an exaggerated stretch reflex response and pathological co-contraction of antagonist muscles during voluntary movement (26, 27).
This spinal-level dysregulation has direct kinematic consequences during reaching. When a stroke survivor attempts elbow extension — a prerequisite for forward reaching — the biceps brachii, if spastic, resists passive lengthening with a velocity-dependent reflex contraction. Simultaneously, reduced reciprocal inhibition means that the biceps is not adequately silenced during voluntary triceps activation, generating pathological co-contraction that opposes elbow extension and fragments the velocity profile into multiple correction-driven submovements. It is precisely this co-contraction and impaired reflex modulation that the elbow opening and muscle quieting block of the AOMI protocol was designed to address through targeted motor imagery.
It is important, however, to recognize that not all resistance to movement in post-stroke patients represents neural spasticity. With chronicity, secondary biomechanical changes — including muscle fibre shortening, increased intramuscular collagen deposition, and reduced sarcomere compliance — produce velocity-independent passive mechanical stiffness that superimposes upon the neural component (6, 7). Clinical differentiation between neural spasticity and mechanical contracture is therefore essential for appropriately targeting rehabilitation interventions.
1.4 Motor Imagery and Action Observation: Neurophysiological Foundations
Given that many stroke survivors, particularly those with severe hemiplegia, cannot generate the voluntary motor output required for task-based physical practice, alternative approaches targeting central neural representations of movement have attracted considerable clinical and scientific interest. Motor Imagery (MI) — defined as the cognitive simulation of a motor action in the absence of overt physical movement — has emerged as one such approach (28).
The neurophysiological rationale for MI in motor rehabilitation rests on the principle of functional equivalence: motor imagery and physical motor execution share overlapping neural substrates, including the premotor cortex, supplementary motor area, posterior parietal cortex, cerebellum, basal ganglia, and primary motor cortex (28, 29). Neuroimaging studies using functional MRI have consistently demonstrated that imagined movements produce cortical activation patterns qualitatively similar to — though quantitatively reduced compared to — those observed during actual execution (29). This sub-threshold cortical activation during MI is accompanied by measurable modulation of corticospinal excitability, as demonstrated by increases in motor-evoked potential amplitude recorded with transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) during upper limb motor imagery (30).
Beyond cortical activation, MI has been shown to exert modulatory effects on spinal reflex excitability. Studies employing H-reflex methodology have demonstrated that upper limb motor imagery produces task-specific changes in spinal interneuronal circuits, including modulation of reciprocal inhibition between antagonist muscle pairs (31). These spinal-level effects suggest that MI may have the capacity to address, at least in part, the reflex dysregulation that underlies spasticity-related movement fragmentation — a mechanism of potential relevance to the kinematic outcomes targeted in the present study.
In stroke survivors, systematic reviews and meta-analyses have indicated that MI training can improve upper limb motor function, particularly when combined with conventional physiotherapy (32). The proposed mechanisms include strengthening of cortical motor representations through repeated sub-threshold activation of corticomotor circuits, facilitation of interhemispheric reorganization by preferentially activating ipsilesional cortex, and modulation of spinal reflex gain through descending modulatory pathways (31, 32).
Action Observation (AO) refers to the visual perception of movement performed by another agent or by oneself via mirror-reversed video. The underlying neural mechanism involves premotor and parietal regions that discharge both during action execution and during observation of the same action — a property first described in non-human primates and subsequently demonstrated in humans using neuroimaging methods (33). Observing skilled, smooth movement provides the brain with a high-fidelity visuomotor template of the desired motor program, activating motor representations without requiring physical execution.
When AO is combined with MI in a sequential AOMI paradigm — observation followed immediately by mental rehearsal — the resulting neural activation is greater and more specific than either modality alone (34). This synergistic effect is thought to occur because AO supplies a rich visuomotor scaffold that refines the subsequent motor image, enabling more accurate and vivid internal simulation (34, 35). For stroke survivors with impoverished internal models of smooth movement due to chronic motor impairment, the provision of an external visuomotor reference through AO before each imagery bout may be particularly important in enabling effective MI.
1.5 The PETTLEP Model: Maximizing Functional Equivalence in Motor Imagery
The effectiveness of motor imagery as a neuroplasticity-inducing intervention depends heavily on the quality and ecological validity of the imagined experience (36). Generic or poorly structured imagery instructions typically produce weak, unfocused, and clinically ineffective simulations. To overcome this limitation, Holmes and Collins (36) proposed the PETTLEP model, a theoretically grounded framework for maximizing functional equivalence between imagined and physically executed movements.
PETTLEP encompasses seven dimensions along which motor imagery should be individualized and structured:
Physical: Adopting the same posture and limb position used during actual task execution.
Environment: Performing imagery in the same environmental context as the physical task.
Task: Using a functionally relevant, specific, and ecologically valid movement.
Timing: Performing imagery in real time, verified via mental chronometry.
Learning: Adapting the imagery progressively according to the individual's current skill level.
Emotion: Incorporating an appropriate affective tone emphasizing ease, fluidity, and confidence to counteract anxiety and spasticity-related hypertonicity.
Perspective: Using an internal (first-person, kinesthetic) perspective, which has been shown to elicit greater corticospinal excitability than external perspectives (36, 37).
Despite its strong theoretical foundation, the application of the PETTLEP model in stroke rehabilitation remains limited, particularly within an AOMI paradigm without interleaved physical practice (37).
1.6 Kinematic Outcome Measurement: Markerless Motion Capture Pipeline
A critical methodological challenge in upper limb rehabilitation research is the objective, high-fidelity quantification of movement quality. Traditional clinical scales such as the Fugl-Meyer Assessment rely on ordinal scoring and cannot capture subtle kinematic signatures — such as velocity profile smoothness or compensatory movement patterns — that differentiate high- from low-quality movement (9). Conventional marker-based motion capture systems, although accurate, are often impractical in clinical settings due to their cost, setup complexity, and required expertise.
A custom Python-based markerless motion capture pipeline using MediaPipe Pose Landmarker (Google) was employed for kinematic data extraction. MediaPipe Pose Landmarker is a machine-learning model that detects 33 body landmarks from a single two-dimensional video feed at approximately 30 frames per second. The pipeline extracts landmark coordinates (x, y, z) along with visibility scores, outputting a 101-column CSV file per trial. Video data are captured using a single smartphone or webcam at 1080p resolution, eliminating the need for multiple cameras or cloud-based processing. A monocular depth estimation network (ZoeDepth, Intel/zoedepth-nyu) is applied to a single video frame to estimate metric shoulder width, which serves as a scaling factor for normalizing kinematic measurements. An optional OpenSim Inverse Kinematics (IK) module, utilizing a custom 14-degree-of-freedom PinJoint model in OpenSim 4.5, can be employed for exploratory joint-angle analysis; both the marker trajectory (TRC) and joint-angle (MOT) data are smoothed with a 15-frame moving-average filter.
In the present study, the following kinematic parameters were to be derived from the MediaPipe-based pipeline:
Movement Duration (total_duration_s): Total duration from onset to offset within the active movement window. Movement onset is defined as the first frame where wrist displacement from rest exceeds a normalized threshold of 0.03 (in shoulder-width units). Movement offset is defined as the frame where instantaneous hand velocity falls below 5% of peak velocity. This hybrid displacement–velocity method produces a single contiguous active window per trial.
Peak and Mean Velocity (total_peak_velocity, total_mean_velocity): Maximum and average instantaneous hand velocity (normalized units/second) within the active movement window, reflecting motor output intensity.
Trunk-to-Palm Displacement Ratio (total_trunk_palm_ratio): Ratio of cumulative trunk displacement to cumulative palm displacement within the active window, indexing compensatory trunk movement during reaching.
Maximum Elbow Angle (total_max_elbow_deg): Peak elbow flexion angle (degrees) during the active movement window, computed from shoulder–elbow–wrist landmarks.
Movement Smoothness (smoothness_pause_pct): Percentage of time within the active window where hand velocity falls below an absolute threshold of 0.03 normalized units — a consistent measure of movement intermittency across participants. Additional metrics include total_path_length (wrist path normalized to shoulder width), total_lat_range_norm (medial–lateral hand range), arm_length_norm (arm length / shoulder width), shoulder_width_norm, shoulder_vert_norm (shoulder vertical displacement range during the active window; cm export as total_depression_cm via ZoeDepth), trunk_lat_norm, and trunk_vert_norm (trunk lateral and forward flexion).
1.7 Rationale and Gap in the Literature
The present study addressed several gaps in the existing literature simultaneously. First, while multi-session AOMI protocols have demonstrated cumulative benefits in stroke rehabilitation, no study had examined the immediate, within-session kinematic response to a single AOMI bout. Second, no prior AOMI trial in stroke had employed the PETTLEP framework to individualize and structure the imagery experience. Third, previous AOMI studies had universally incorporated interleaved physical practice, making it impossible to isolate the specific cognitive-motor effects of observation and imagery from motor practice effects. Fourth, no study had used a single-camera MediaPipe-based markerless kinematic pipeline to quantify the kinematic effects of AOMI in stroke survivors, relying instead on ordinal clinical scales that lack the sensitivity to detect subtle within-session changes in movement trajectory quality.
The present study addressed these gaps by delivering a single session of PETTLEP-based AOMI — incorporating a sensorimotor transfer calibration phase, individualized mirror-reversed first-person observation, and progressive corrective motor imagery targeting initiation, postural control, and smoothness — and measuring the immediate kinematic response using the custom MediaPipe-based markerless motion capture pipeline with monocular depth estimation. This trial was designed to provide the first direct, objective evidence for the acute neuromotor effects of this approach in persons post-stroke.

2. METHODS
Study Design
This study employed a single-blind, pretest–posttest, prospective randomized controlled trial (RCT) design with two parallel arms: an experimental condition (PETTLEP-based AOMI) and a cognitive and somatic control condition. The design and reporting followed the CONSORT 2010 guidelines for non-pharmacological interventions.
The trial was to be conducted at the Neurorehabilitation Clinic of Istinye University Liv Bahçeşehir Hospital, Istanbul, Türkiye. The study was to commence after obtaining approval from the Istinye University Institutional Ethics Committee and was to be prospectively registered at ClinicalTrials.gov prior to recruitment. All procedures were to be performed in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. Written informed consent was to be obtained from all participants before any study-related procedures.
The total study duration, from ethics approval through recruitment, data collection, and analysis, was estimated at 12 months (February 2026 to February 2027).
Participants
The study population consisted of stroke survivors referred to or followed at the Neurorehabilitation Clinic of Istinye University Liv Bahçeşehir Hospital. Participants were to be identified through clinical record review and direct referrals from neurologists and physiotherapists. All potential participants were to undergo eligibility screening by a neurologist and a physiotherapist.
Participants were to receive a detailed verbal and written explanation of the study purpose, procedures, potential risks, and their right to withdraw at any time without affecting their clinical care. Written informed consent was to be obtained prior to any study procedures.
Inclusion Criteria
Male or female adults aged 40–80 years.
First-ever unilateral ischemic or hemorrhagic stroke confirmed by CT or MRI, or recurrent stroke with no residual motor sequelae from previous events.
MRC muscle strength grade ≥ 2 in the affected upper limb.
Mild to moderate spasticity (Modified Ashworth Scale ≤ 2).
Sufficient cognitive function to understand simple instructions and perform brief motor imagery tasks (as judged clinically by the examiner).
Medically stable with no active or uncontrolled systemic illness.
Ability to sit unsupported for at least 30 minutes.
Ability to perform the brief cognitive-motor exercises required by the protocol.
Exclusion Criteria
Other central nervous system disorders besides stroke (e.g., Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, traumatic brain injury).
Musculoskeletal or orthopedic conditions restricting upper limb movement, including fixed contractures, severe joint deformities, or clinically detectable glenohumeral subluxation (> 1 finger breadth by palpation).
Moderate to severe sensory loss (NIHSS sensory score ≥ 2) or moderate to severe neglect (NIHSS extinction/inattention score ≥ 2).
Uncontrolled seizures, severe cardiovascular instability, or any contraindication to imagery-based interventions.
Withdrawal Criteria
Participants were to be withdrawn if any exclusion criterion was identified after randomization, if an orthopedic injury or acute medical event prevented continuation, or if the participant voluntarily withdrew consent.
Sample Size Calculation
Sample size was estimated a priori using G*Power version 3.1.9.2 for a 2 (Group) × 2 (Time) repeated-measures ANOVA, with the primary outcome being the Group × Time interaction effect on movement smoothness (smoothness_pause_pct).
The effect size was based on comparable single-session motor imagery studies reporting Cohen's d values of 1.0–1.4 for kinematic outcomes, converted to f = 0.40. This assumption was acknowledged to be somewhat optimistic given the acute, single-session AOMI design. With α = 0.05, power = 0.95, and correlation between repeated measures r = 0.50, the minimum sample size was calculated as 24 participants (12 per group). Allowing for up to 15% dropout, the target recruitment sample was set at 28 participants (14 per group).
Randomization and Allocation Concealment
Participants were to be randomized in a 1:1 ratio using permuted-block randomization stratified by sex and spasticity severity (mild: MAS 0–1+; moderate: MAS 2). An independent investigator not involved in the study was to generate the randomization sequence using www.randomizer.org with variable block sizes. Allocation concealment was to be maintained using sequentially numbered opaque sealed envelopes (SNOSE).
Due to the nature of the intervention, participants could not be blinded. However, the outcome assessor was to remain blinded to group allocation. Kinematic analysis was performed using the standardized automated NeuroLab pipeline (kinematics_analyzer.py v6), which applies identical segmentation and metric extraction algorithms to all trials regardless of group assignment.
Assessment Procedures
All assessments were to be conducted in a standardized environment at the Neurorehabilitation Unit. Each participant was to be assessed immediately before and immediately after the intervention session.
Standardization measures included use of identical dark long-sleeve clothing, secured hair for clear landmark visibility, consistent chair positioning, avoidance of other upper limb therapy on assessment days, and delivery of all instructions from a standardized script by the same blinded physiotherapist.
Task Execution Protocol
The assessment and intervention task was the Reach & Wipe — an in-air reaching and wiping motion using the affected upper limb. This task was selected for its ecological validity and suitability for markerless tracking. Standardized verbal instructions were to be provided without specific speed cues to capture natural movement behavior. Participants were to perform three trials at each assessment point, with the mean used for analysis.
Video Data Collection Setup
Kinematic data were to be recorded using a single smartphone or webcam positioned at a standardized frontal view at 1080p resolution and 30 fps with consistent diffuse lighting. Participants wore dark, form-fitting long-sleeve clothing with secured hair and exposed wrists to facilitate landmark tracking, without reflective markers. Videos were processed offline using the custom Python-based MediaPipe pipeline on a local workstation — no internet connection or cloud upload was required.
Kinematic variables were to be extracted using the custom Python analysis pipeline (kinematics_analyzer.py, version 6) as follows:
Movement Onset and Offset: Onset is detected when wrist displacement from rest exceeds 0.03 normalized threshold. Offset is detected when hand velocity falls below 5% of peak velocity. These events define a single active movement window.
Auto Limb Detection: The active (affected) side is automatically identified as the limb with the longer cumulative wrist path length, eliminating manual side specification.
Full-Recording Metrics: All metrics — total_duration_s, total_peak_velocity, total_mean_velocity, total_path_length, total_lat_range_norm, total_trunk_palm_ratio, total_max_elbow_deg, smoothness_pause_pct, arm_length_norm, shoulder_width_norm, total_depression_cm, trunk_lat_norm, trunk_vert_norm — are computed within the single active movement window.
ZoeDepth Metric Scaling: The ZoeDepth monocular depth estimation network (Intel/zoedepth-nyu) is applied to a single video frame to estimate shoulder width in meters, used to scale normalized measurements to real-world units (e.g., shoulder vertical range in cm, exported as total_depression_cm).
OpenSim Inverse Kinematics (Exploratory): An OpenSim 4.5 model with 14 PinJoint degrees of freedom is used for complementary joint-angle analysis. TRC and MOT data are both smoothed with a 15-frame moving-average filter.

2.7 Intervention Protocol
2.7.1 Overview
The intervention consisted of a single 22-minute session comprising a 2-minute sensorimotor transfer calibration phase followed by five 4-minute blocks (20 minutes total), administered immediately following the pre-intervention assessment. Both groups received the intervention in the same environmental setting.
2.7.2 Experimental Group: PETTLEP-Based AOMI
The experimental protocol employed an AOMI paradigm — observation followed immediately by imagery, without interleaved physical execution. The decision to exclude physical practice was deliberate, serving two purposes: (1) to isolate the specific cognitive-motor effects of observation and imagery from any motor or sensory effects of physical practice; and (2) to reduce the physical and perceptual burden on participants, particularly those with severe motor impairment.
The intervention began with a 2-minute Sensorimotor Transfer Calibration phase, during which participants were taught a "notice–name–transfer" strategy. They were instructed to attend to a specific kinesthetic sensation in the unaffected limb (e.g., smoothness, stability, relaxation), label it with a single-word cue, and then mentally transfer that sensation to the affected limb. This phase also included supported limb awareness and surface-contact anchoring to enhance sensorimotor engagement without physical resistance or active movement.
Following calibration, the intervention proceeded through five 4-minute blocks, each structured as follows:
Minute 1 — Action Observation: The participant watched a mirror-reversed, first-person perspective video of their own unaffected upper limb performing the Reach & Wipe movement. The video was horizontally flipped so that it appeared to show the affected side — providing a personalized, anatomically congruent visual model.
Minutes 2–3 — Corrective Motor Imagery: The participant closed their eyes and performed guided kinesthetic motor imagery of the same Reach & Wipe movement as performed by the affected arm. Standardized audio cues delivered via headphones guided the participant to imagine the movement while applying the target corrective constraint for that block. If the imagined movement became jerky, effortful, or associated with trunk or shoulder compensation, the participant was instructed to mentally return to the last smooth and correct point and continue with the corrected motor plan.
Minute 4 — Rest: A quiet rest period with eyes open, during which the participant was instructed to relax and to avoid voluntary movement or mental imagery.
The five blocks progressively targeted specific kinematic components, ordered in accordance with the proximal-to-distal principle of normal motor control — feedforward postural stabilization before distal movement execution:
Block 1 — Movement Initiation Transfer: The participant observed the video with attention directed toward the calm, clear start of the movement. During imagery, the participant was instructed to transfer the feeling of an easy, clear start from the unaffected limb to the affected limb, imagining the brain sending a simple "start now" signal without hesitation or rush.
Block 2 — Trunk Stability Transfer: The participant observed the video with attention directed toward the quiet, stable trunk. During imagery, the participant transferred the feeling of a steady, grounded trunk to the affected side, imagining the arm reaching forward while the trunk remained still and supported. This block was grounded in the neurophysiological principle that the central nervous system normally stabilizes the trunk via anticipatory postural adjustments before initiating voluntary arm movement (20); targeting this representation in imagery was intended to reinstate feedforward postural control at the cortical level.
Block 3 — Shoulder Girdle Control Transfer: The participant observed the video with attention directed toward the low, relaxed shoulder position. During imagery, the participant transferred the feeling of a calm, heavy shoulder to the affected side, imagining the shoulder staying low and away from the ear throughout the reach.
Block 4 — Elbow Opening and Muscle Sensation Transfer: The participant observed the video with attention directed toward the easy opening of the elbow and quiet muscle work. During imagery, the participant transferred the feeling of smooth elbow extension and relaxed muscle effort to the affected side, imagining the elbow opening gently without stiffness or resistance. This block specifically targeted the imagery of reduced co-contraction and antagonist quieting — processes that correspond neurophysiologically to the restoration of reciprocal inhibition between elbow flexors and extensors (27).
Block 5 — Integrated Smooth Movement: The participant observed the video once more. During imagery, all corrective elements were integrated: clear start, stable trunk, relaxed shoulder, smooth elbow opening, and continuous hand trajectory. The participant imagined the complete Reach & Wipe as a unified, effortless, and kinematically correct movement at natural timing.
This structure produced a total intervention dose of:
5 minutes of action observation
10 minutes of corrective motor imagery
5 minutes of rest
2 minutes of sensorimotor transfer calibration
Total: 22 minutes
Intervention Fidelity:All audio cues were to be delivered via pre-recorded standardized digital audio files to ensure exact replication across participants and sessions. Mental chronometry verification was to be conducted after each imagery block: the participant would be asked "Did the imagined movement feel like it happened at the same pace as you would normally move?" If the participant reported significant temporal distortion (e.g., imagining the movement as instantaneous), the physiotherapist would provide corrective guidance before the next block.
The supervising physiotherapist was to complete a structured fidelity checklist after each block, recording: participant engagement (attention maintained), compliance with eyes-closed instruction during imagery, absence of overt movement, and any adverse events or complaints.
2.7.3 Control Group: Cognitive and Somatic Control
The control condition was designed to match the AOMI protocol in: (a) total session duration (22 minutes); (b) physical setup (same room, chair, tablet present); (c) use of headphones and audio delivery; and (d) level of overall cognitive engagement — while specifically avoiding activation of the sensorimotor or premotor cortex, to serve as an active cognitive control for non-specific attention and engagement effects.
The control protocol consisted of 5 blocks × 4 minutes = 20 minutes, preceded by a 2-minute introductory relaxation phase, alternating between two tasks:
Body Scanning (Somatic Attention): The participant was guided through a structured body scan, directing attention sequentially from the feet to the head, noting sensations such as warmth, pressure, or the feeling of the chair, without any movement or imagery of movement.
Spatial Navigation (Visuospatial Mental Simulation): The participant was asked to mentally walk through a familiar environment (their own home or a familiar route), visualizing the spatial layout, the placement of furniture, or the sequence of rooms. This task engaged visuospatial and hippocampal networks but did not activate sensorimotor circuits, because the imagined content did not involve human limb movement.
Both tasks were to be delivered via standardized audio recordings through headphones.
2.7.4 Implementation of the PETTLEP Framework: Detailed Exposition
The seven PETTLEP dimensions were operationalized in the present protocol as summarized in Table 1.
Table 1. Operationalization of PETTLEP dimensions in the present intervention protocol.
PETTLEP Dimension | Operationalization in This Study
P – Physical | Participants seated in identical posture to assessment; limb placement replicated starting configuration of Reach & Wipe task
E – Environment | Same room, chair, lighting, and spatial layout as assessment; tablet at standardized height and distance
T – Task | Reach & Wipe — in-air reaching and wiping movement; imagery constraints targeted initiation, smoothness, and trunk-limb dissociation

2.8 Statistical Analysis
All statistical analyses were to be performed using IBM SPSS Statistics for Mac, version 24.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, New York, USA). The level of statistical significance for all analyses was set at α = 0.05 (two-tailed).
2.8.1 Descriptive Statistics
Continuous variables were to be reported as mean ± standard deviation (SD) for normally distributed variables, and as median with interquartile range (IQR) for non-normally distributed or ordinal variables. Categorical variables were to be reported as frequency and percentage. The normality of distribution for all continuous variables was to be evaluated using the Shapiro–Wilk test.
Baseline equivalence between groups was to be assessed using independent samples t-tests for continuous variables, chi-square tests for categorical variables, and Mann-Whitney U tests for ordinal variables.
2.8.2 Primary and Secondary Outcome Analysis
Parametric Analysis:
For continuous variables satisfying the normality assumption — including the primary kinematic outcomes (smoothness_pause_pct, total_trunk_palm_ratio, total_depression_cm, total_duration_s, total_peak_velocity) and WMFT-4 scores — treatment effects were to be examined using a 2 × 2 Mixed-Model Analysis of Variance (ANOVA), with:
Within-subject factor: Time (Pre-intervention vs. Post-intervention)
Between-subject factor: Group (Experimental [AOMI] vs. Control)
The primary analysis focused on the Group × Time interaction effect, which tested whether the magnitude of change from pre- to post-intervention differed significantly between the two groups. The main effect of Time indicated whether overall change occurred across both groups. The main effect of Group tested for baseline differences in trajectory.
Non-Parametric Analysis:
For continuous variables found to significantly deviate from normality and resistant to transformation, or for ordinal variables (VAS, VAMS-4, KVIQ-10 scores, Motor Difference Rating Scale (MDRS)), non-parametric equivalents were to be applied:
Between-group comparisons: Mann–Whitney U Test
Within-group pre–post comparisons: Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test
2.8.3 Correlation Analyses
The relationship between participant-rated motor imagery ability (KVIQ-10 visual and kinesthetic subscale scores) and the magnitude of kinematic improvement (Δ smoothness_pause_pct, Δ total_trunk_palm_ratio, Δ total_duration_s) was to be examined using:
Pearson's correlation (r) for normally distributed variables
Spearman's rho (ρ) for non-normally distributed variables
Additionally, the relationship between pre–post changes in VAMS-4 mood scores and kinematic change scores was to be explored to determine whether affective response to the intervention mediated kinematic change.
2.8.4 Effect Size Estimation
To quantify the magnitude of treatment effects:
Partial eta squared (ηp²) for ANOVA results: ηp² 0.01–0.06 = small; 0.06–0.14 = medium; ≥ 0.14 = large
Cohen's d for pairwise comparisons: d 0.20–0.49 = small; 0.50–0.79 = medium; ≥ 0.80 = large
Rank-biserial correlation (r = Z / √N) for non-parametric pairwise tests
2.8.5 Handling of Missing Data
Analyses were to be conducted on an intention-to-treat (ITT) basis, including all randomized participants regardless of protocol adherence. Missing data were not to be deleted (no pairwise or listwise deletion). Instead, missing values were to be imputed using:
Last Observation Carried Forward (LOCF) as the primary imputation method
Linear Mixed Models (LMM) as a sensitivity analysis to test the robustness of findings to the imputation approach
3. EXPECTED RESULTS
Note: As this is a prospective study protocol, actual results are not yet available. The following section presents the analytical framework, the hypothesized direction of findings, and the expected clinical significance of the results, consistent with the conventions of prospective trial protocol manuscripts.
3.1 Primary Outcome: Movement Smoothness (smoothness_pause_pct)
Based on the neurophysiological rationale presented above and the effect sizes reported in comparable single-session motor imagery studies, it was hypothesized that:
The AOMI group would demonstrate a statistically significant reduction in smoothness_pause_pct from pre- to post-intervention (within-group effect).
The control group would demonstrate no significant change or a minimal, non-clinically meaningful change in smoothness_pause_pct.
The Group × Time interaction effect would be statistically significant (p < 0.05), with a medium-to-large effect size (ηp² ≥ 0.14).
A reduction in smoothness_pause_pct would indicate that the AOMI session facilitated a shift toward smoother, more continuous reaching trajectories — consistent with the hypothesis that PETTLEP-based motor imagery, primed by action observation, promotes the neural planning of more efficient movement.
3.2 Secondary Kinematic Outcomes
It was further hypothesized that:
Total Movement Duration: The AOMI group would demonstrate a significant reduction in total_duration_s, reflecting more efficient movement execution facilitated by the initiation-focused imagery constraint in Block 1. No significant change was expected in the control group.
Trunk-to-Palm Ratio: The AOMI group would demonstrate a significant reduction in total_trunk_palm_ratio during reaching, reflecting reduced reliance on compensatory trunk strategies as a result of the trunk stability imagery constraint in Block 2 — which was specifically designed to reinstate the internal representation of anticipatory postural stabilization before arm movement. No significant change was expected in the control group.
Shoulder Vertical Displacement Range: The AOMI group would demonstrate a significant reduction in shoulder_vert_norm (and its cm equivalent, total_depression_cm), reflecting less compensatory shoulder girdle movement during reaching, consistent with the shoulder heaviness imagery instruction in Block 3. No significant change was expected in the control group.
Peak Hand Velocity: A significant increase in total_peak_velocity was anticipated in the AOMI group, reflecting more confident motor output facilitated by the observation-then-imagery paradigm.
3.3 Upper Limb Functional Performance (WMFT-4)
Given the acute, single-session nature of the intervention, any WMFT-4 changes were expected to be modest. However, a trend toward improvement in the AOMI group, reflecting transfer of the kinematically improved movement quality to standardized functional tasks, was anticipated. Whether a single session was sufficient to produce clinically meaningful WMFT-4 changes — equivalent to the established minimal clinically important difference — remained an important empirical question that this trial was designed to address.
3.4 Moderator Analyses
It was hypothesized that participants with higher baseline KVIQ scores (greater motor imagery ability) would demonstrate larger kinematic improvements in the AOMI group (negative correlation between imagery ability (KVIQ-10) and Δ smoothness_pause_pct (i.e., greater imagery ability associated with larger reductions in pause percentage)), providing evidence for the individual-difference moderating role of imagery capacity. No such relationship was expected in the control group.
Additionally, participants reporting greater positive mood (VAMS-4 happy/calm subscales) post-intervention may have demonstrated larger kinematic improvements, consistent with the emotion-performance facilitation hypothesis embedded within the PETTLEP model.
4. DISCUSSION
4.1 Significance and Novelty of the Study
The present trial addressed several critical gaps in the neurorehabilitation literature simultaneously, making it a methodologically and conceptually novel contribution on multiple fronts.
First, this was the first study designed to investigate the immediate, within-session kinematic effects of a single AOMI intervention in stroke survivors. Existing evidence for AOMI in stroke was based predominantly on multi-session, multi-week training protocols, which — while demonstrating cumulative benefits — provided no information about the acute neuromotor response that may drive early changes. Understanding acute effects was of fundamental importance for optimizing session structure, dosing, and scheduling of AOMI within comprehensive rehabilitation programs.
Second, this was the first study to employ a custom markerless motion capture pipeline based on MediaPipe Pose Landmarker as the primary outcome measurement technology in an AOMI stroke rehabilitation trial. The use of objective, continuous kinematic data — rather than ordinal clinical scales — enabled detection of subtle, within-session changes in movement quality that would not have been captured by conventional assessment tools. Specifically, the smoothness metric (smoothness_pause_pct) as a primary outcome was sensitive to improvements in motor control at the level of movement trajectory planning, which was the precise neural process targeted by the PETTLEP-AOMI protocol.
Third, the present study employed an AOMI paradigm without interleaved physical practice — allowing isolation of the specific cognitive-motor effects of observation and imagery. This was methodologically important because all previous AOMI studies in stroke had incorporated physical practice components, making it impossible to attribute observed effects to the imagery and observation components specifically.
Fourth, the application of the PETTLEP framework to AOMI delivery in stroke represented a theoretical advance over existing AOMI protocols, which had typically employed standardized video stimuli and generic imagery instructions without individualization. The PETTLEP model's emphasis on ecological validity, postural congruence, real-time timing, and internal perspective was expected to maximize the functional equivalence — and hence neuroplastic efficacy — of the imagined movement.
4.2 Interpretive Framework: Mechanisms of Expected Effects
The expected kinematic improvements in the AOMI group could be interpreted within a coherent neurophysiological framework:
Action Observation activates motor representations: The mirror-reversed video of the unaffected limb performing a smooth Reach & Wipe engaged the participant's sensorimotor circuits, activating the motor representations of reaching in premotor and parietal cortices (33). Because the video was mirror-reversed, it provided a visually congruent model of the affected limb — increasing the specificity of neural activation to the paretic side's motor programs.
Motor Imagery consolidates and refines the motor plan: Immediately following observation, the participant mentally rehearsed the movement they had just observed, imagining it as performed by their affected arm. This sequential observation-then-imagery cycle was proposed to operate as an iterative motor plan calibration process: each observation updated the internal model of the target movement, and each imagery bout rehearsed the updated plan, gradually reducing the discrepancy between the actual and desired movement trajectory. Neuroimaging evidence has confirmed that this type of sequential AOMI paradigm produces greater activation of motor planning regions than either modality alone (34).
Sensorimotor transfer enhances cortical specificity: The notice–name–transfer strategy was designed to strengthen functional equivalence by explicitly mapping the kinesthetic experience of the unaffected limb onto the affected limb's sensorimotor representation. By transferring specific sensations (easy start, stable trunk, calm shoulder, smooth elbow), the imagery protocol aimed to facilitate cross-limb integration of motor programs, manifesting as reduced smoothness_pause_pct, reduced total_trunk_palm_ratio, and reduced compensatory shoulder elevation in the post-intervention assessment.
Imagery of trunk stability reinstates feedforward postural control: The trunk stability imagery in Block 2 was specifically designed to reinstate, at the level of cortical motor representation, the feedforward postural organization that normally precedes arm movement. By imagining the trunk as stable and grounded before the arm reaches forward, the participant rehearsed the APA-first, arm-action-second sequence that characterizes normal reaching (20). This cortical rehearsal of the anticipatory postural hierarchy was hypothesized to reduce compensatory trunk lean in the subsequent physical assessment.
Imagery of reciprocal muscle quieting targets spinal reflex dysregulation: The elbow opening and muscle quieting imagery in Block 4 specifically addressed the co-contraction and impaired reciprocal inhibition that underlie movement fragmentation in spastic hemiparesis (27). By imagining smooth elbow extension with relaxed antagonist musculature, the participant rehearsed the neural pattern of agonist activation with antagonist inhibition — a pattern that MI has been shown to reinforce through modulation of spinal interneuronal circuits (31).
Real-time timing preserves temporal motor coding: The PETTLEP timing component — imagining the movement in real time — preserved the temporal structure of the motor program, ensuring that the rehearsed neural patterns matched the actual timing requirements of the movement. This was particularly important for smoothness_pause_pct and total_duration_s, because the temporal dynamics of velocity generation were intimately linked to the timing of motor planning sub-commands.
4.3 The Role of Emotional State in Intervention Response
The inclusion of the VAMS-4 as an affective outcome measure reflected recognition that emotional state may serve as an important moderator of AOMI efficacy. Anxiety and negative affect are known to increase muscle tone and co-contraction — potentially counteracting the relaxation-promoting imagery cues — and to reduce cognitive resource allocation for imagery vividness. Conversely, positive affect may facilitate more vivid, stable, and functionally equivalent motor imagery (36).
The PETTLEP model's emotion dimension — operationalized in this study through audio cues emphasizing ease, fluidity, and relaxation — was specifically designed to promote positive affect and reduce performance anxiety during imagery. By measuring VAMS-4 scores before and after the intervention, this study was able to examine whether pre-intervention affective state moderated kinematic response, and whether the AOMI protocol itself induced positive affective change.
4.4 Methodological Considerations and Potential Limitations
Several methodological considerations merit discussion:
Sample Size and Statistical Power: The target sample of 28 participants was powered for a medium-to-large Group × Time interaction effect (f = 0.40). While this effect size was based on comparable acute MI studies, it was acknowledged to be optimistic given the novelty of the AOMI paradigm. Should the actual effect size be smaller, the study may have been underpowered, and results should be interpreted accordingly. Future large-scale studies should be planned based on the effect size estimates generated by this trial.
Single-Session Design: The acute design was both a strength (enabling isolation of immediate neuromotor effects) and a potential limitation (single-session improvements may not persist beyond the post-intervention assessment). Future trials should include follow-up assessments (e.g., 24 hours, 1 week post-intervention) to examine persistence of effects and to characterize the trajectory of kinematic improvement.
MediaPipe Tracking Accuracy in Post-Stroke Populations: While MediaPipe Pose Landmarker has been validated for general human pose estimation, its accuracy in stroke survivors — who may present with atypical movement patterns, variable clothing, and spasticity-related postural abnormalities — had not been specifically validated for this clinical population. Potential sources of tracking error included occlusion of landmarks during compensatory trunk lean, and AI landmark misidentification in non-standard body configurations. Standardization procedures (consistent clothing, controlled lighting, fixed camera positions) were intended to mitigate these risks; however, a formal within-study reliability analysis was to be performed.
Control Condition Selection: The cognitive and somatic control condition (body scanning + spatial navigation) was designed to control for non-specific effects of attention, time, and interaction with a physiotherapist. However, it could not fully isolate the specific neuromotor effects of AOMI from potential effects of general cognitive engagement, relaxation, or affect regulation induced by the body scan. Future studies might consider a more specific active control — for example, a verbal semantic task matched in difficulty and engagement — to further improve experimental specificity.
Blinding Limitations: Participants could not be blinded to their group assignment due to the fundamentally different nature of the experimental and control conditions. This introduced a risk of demand characteristics and performance expectancy bias. While the blinded outcome assessor mitigated clinical assessment bias and kinematic extraction was fully automated (NeuroLab pipeline), participant-level expectancy effects could not be fully eliminated.
4.5 Clinical Implications
If the hypothesized results were confirmed, the findings of this trial would have several important practical implications for stroke rehabilitation:
Integration into Early Rehabilitation: A 22-minute, single-session PETTLEP-based AOMI intervention requiring only a tablet and headphones is highly feasible for integration into the early rehabilitation phase, even in individuals with severe motor impairment who cannot participate in task-based physical training.
Personalization via Mirror-Reversed Video: The use of each participant's own unaffected limb as the action observation model represented a personalized and immediately available source of relevant movement information, requiring no additional expertise or resources beyond the recording equipment already available in most clinical settings.
The markerless motion capture pipeline requires only a single smartphone or webcam and a standard laptop for offline processing, making it feasible for implementation in resource-limited rehabilitation settings without requiring internet connectivity.
Dosing and Optimization: This trial's findings regarding the magnitude and specificity of acute kinematic effects would inform the optimal dosing, frequency, and combination of AOMI sessions within comprehensive rehabilitation programs.
Patient-Reported Outcomes: The inclusion of the Motor Difference Rating Scale provided a patient-reported measure of perceived benefit, which is increasingly recognized as an important complement to objective kinematic assessment in documenting the clinical value of rehabilitation interventions.
5. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
The present randomized controlled trial protocol described a methodologically rigorous, theoretically grounded, and clinically relevant investigation of the immediate kinematic effects of a single session of PETTLEP-based AOMI in stroke survivors. By combining individualized PETTLEP imagery principles — ordered in accordance with the proximal-to-distal hierarchy of normal feedforward motor control — with a sequential AOMI paradigm without physical practice, and measuring outcomes with a custom MediaPipe-based markerless motion capture pipeline integrating monocular depth estimation (ZoeDepth) and optional OpenSim inverse kinematics, this trial was positioned to make a substantive contribution to the evidence base for cognitively mediated neurorehabilitation.
The study was designed to provide the first direct, objective kinematic evidence addressing whether a single AOMI session could induce measurable improvements in movement smoothness (smoothness_pause_pct), trunk compensation, shoulder girdle elevation, movement time, and functional upper limb performance in post-stroke individuals, compared to an active cognitive and somatic control.
Beyond the immediate findings, this trial was designed to:
Establish proof-of-concept for the kinematic measurability of acute AOMI effects in stroke, enabling sample size planning for future multi-session efficacy trials.
Generate a dataset of pre- and post-intervention two-dimensional pose-landmark kinematics (with metric-scaled derivatives) from stroke survivors performing an ecologically valid upper limb task, to be made available to the research community via open data repositories.
Demonstrate the feasibility of a MediaPipe-based markerless motion capture pipeline as a kinematic outcome measurement tool in a clinical neurorehabilitation setting.
Explore the moderating roles of imagery ability (KVIQ-10), affective state (VAMS-4), and physical activity level (IPAQ) in determining individual AOMI responsiveness, informing personalized prescription of imagery-based rehabilitation.
Future research should build upon the findings of this trial to investigate: (a) the optimal number of AOMI sessions required to produce clinically durable kinematic improvements; (b) the additive or synergistic effects of combining AOMI with physical therapy or neurostimulation; (c) the neural mechanisms underlying AOMI-induced kinematic change, using neuroimaging or neurophysiological (TMS, EEG) outcome measures; and (d) the generalizability of the PETTLEP-AOMI approach to other functional upper limb tasks (e.g., grasping, object manipulation) and to individuals at different stages of stroke recovery.
