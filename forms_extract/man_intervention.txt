134|2.7 Intervention Protocol
135|2.7.1 Overview
136|The intervention consisted of a single 22-minute session comprising a 2-minute sensorimotor transfer calibration phase followed by five 4-minute blocks (20 minutes total), administered immediately following the pre-intervention assessment. Both groups received the intervention in the same environmental setting.
137|2.7.2 Experimental Group: PETTLEP-Based AOMI
138|The experimental protocol employed an AOMI paradigm — observation followed immediately by imagery, without interleaved physical execution. The decision to exclude physical practice was deliberate, serving two purposes: (1) to isolate the specific cognitive-motor effects of observation and imagery from any motor or sensory effects of physical practice; and (2) to reduce the physical and perceptual burden on participants, particularly those with severe motor impairment.
139|The intervention began with a 2-minute Sensorimotor Transfer Calibration phase, during which participants were taught a "notice–name–transfer" strategy. They were instructed to attend to a specific kinesthetic sensation in the unaffected limb (e.g., smoothness, stability, relaxation), label it with a single-word cue, and then mentally transfer that sensation to the affected limb. This phase also included supported limb awareness and surface-contact anchoring to enhance sensorimotor engagement without physical resistance or active movement.
140|Following calibration, the intervention proceeded through five 4-minute blocks, each structured as follows:
141|Minute 1 — Action Observation: The participant watched a mirror-reversed, first-person perspective video of their own unaffected upper limb performing the Reach & Wipe movement. The video was horizontally flipped so that it appeared to show the affected side — providing a personalized, anatomically congruent visual model.
142|Minutes 2–3 — Corrective Motor Imagery: The participant closed their eyes and performed guided kinesthetic motor imagery of the same Reach & Wipe movement as performed by the affected arm. Standardized audio cues delivered via headphones guided the participant to imagine the movement while applying the target corrective constraint for that block. If the imagined movement became jerky, effortful, or associated with trunk or shoulder compensation, the participant was instructed to mentally return to the last smooth and correct point and continue with the corrected motor plan.
143|Minute 4 — Rest: A quiet rest period with eyes open, during which the participant was instructed to relax and to avoid voluntary movement or mental imagery.
144|The five blocks progressively targeted specific kinematic components, ordered in accordance with the proximal-to-distal principle of normal motor control — feedforward postural stabilization before distal movement execution:
145|Block 1 — Movement Initiation Transfer: The participant observed the video with attention directed toward the calm, clear start of the movement. During imagery, the participant was instructed to transfer the feeling of an easy, clear start from the unaffected limb to the affected limb, imagining the brain sending a simple "start now" signal without hesitation or rush.
146|Block 2 — Trunk Stability Transfer: The participant observed the video with attention directed toward the quiet, stable trunk. During imagery, the participant transferred the feeling of a steady, grounded trunk to the affected side, imagining the arm reaching forward while the trunk remained still and supported. This block was grounded in the neurophysiological principle that the central nervous system normally stabilizes the trunk via anticipatory postural adjustments before initiating voluntary arm movement (20); targeting this representation in imagery was intended to reinstate feedforward postural control at the cortical level.
147|Block 3 — Shoulder Girdle Control Transfer: The participant observed the video with attention directed toward the low, relaxed shoulder position. During imagery, the participant transferred the feeling of a calm, heavy shoulder to the affected side, imagining the shoulder staying low and away from the ear throughout the reach.
148|Block 4 — Elbow Opening and Muscle Sensation Transfer: The participant observed the video with attention directed toward the easy opening of the elbow and quiet muscle work. During imagery, the participant transferred the feeling of smooth elbow extension and relaxed muscle effort to the affected side, imagining the elbow opening gently without stiffness or resistance. This block specifically targeted the imagery of reduced co-contraction and antagonist quieting — processes that correspond neurophysiologically to the restoration of reciprocal inhibition between elbow flexors and extensors (27).
149|Block 5 — Integrated Smooth Movement: The participant observed the video once more. During imagery, all corrective elements were integrated: clear start, stable trunk, relaxed shoulder, smooth elbow opening, and continuous hand trajectory. The participant imagined the complete Reach & Wipe as a unified, effortless, and kinematically correct movement at natural timing.
150|This structure produced a total intervention dose of:
151|5 minutes of action observation
152|10 minutes of corrective motor imagery
153|5 minutes of rest
154|2 minutes of sensorimotor transfer calibration
155|Total: 22 minutes
156|Intervention Fidelity:
All audio cues were to be delivered via pre-recorded standardized digital audio files to ensure exact replication across participants and sessions. Mental chronometry verification was to be conducted after each imagery block: the participant would be asked "Did the imagined movement feel like it happened at the same pace as you would normally move?" If the participant reported significant temporal distortion (e.g., imagining the movement as instantaneous), the physiotherapist would provide corrective guidance before the next block.
157|The supervising physiotherapist was to complete a structured fidelity checklist after each block, recording: participant engagement (attention maintained), compliance with eyes-closed instruction during imagery, absence of overt movement, and any adverse events or complaints.
158|2.7.3 Control Group: Cognitive and Somatic Control
159|The control condition was designed to match the AOMI protocol in: (a) total session duration (22 minutes); (b) physical setup (same room, chair, tablet present); (c) use of headphones and audio delivery; and (d) level of overall cognitive engagement — while specifically avoiding activation of the sensorimotor or premotor cortex, to serve as an active cognitive control for non-specific attention and engagement effects.
160|The control protocol consisted of 5 blocks × 4 minutes = 20 minutes, preceded by a 2-minute introductory relaxation phase, alternating between two tasks:
161|Body Scanning (Somatic Attention): The participant was guided through a structured body scan, directing attention sequentially from the feet to the head, noting sensations such as warmth, pressure, or the feeling of the chair, without any movement or imagery of movement.
162|Spatial Navigation (Visuospatial Mental Simulation): The participant was asked to mentally walk through a familiar environment (their own home or a familiar route), visualizing the spatial layout, the placement of furniture, or the sequence of rooms. This task engaged visuospatial and hippocampal networks but did not activate sensorimotor circuits, because the imagined content did not involve human limb movement.
163|Both tasks were to be delivered via standardized audio recordings through headphones.
